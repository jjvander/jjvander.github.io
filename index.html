Graduate Thesis
M.S. Thesis — University of Hawaiʻi at Mānoa, 2021

High Resolution Modeling of Hurricane Impacts in the Hawaiian Islands Under Present and Future Climate Conditions: A Case Study of 2018 Hurricane Lane

Hurricane Lane (2018) was one of the most significant tropical cyclones in Hawaiian history — a Category 5 storm that never made landfall yet delivered over 52 inches of rainfall to the Big Island, making it the second-wettest tropical cyclone ever recorded in the United States. This thesis used the WRF-ARW (Weather Research and Forecasting) model to simulate Lane's impacts at high resolution, conducting an extensive validation process using ensemble runs and applying the Cohen's Kappa statistical method — a novel approach for comparing station observations to gridded model output.

After identifying an optimal model configuration (WSM3 microphysics, RRTM longwave, CAM shortwave, spectral nudging), Pseudo-Global Warming (PGW) experiments were run to project how Lane's impacts might differ under a high-emissions end-of-century climate scenario. The results showed a storm tracking further south of the islands, producing rainfall totals over 200mm drier than the historical simulation — illustrating how changing background conditions could fundamentally alter the threat profile of Hawaiian hurricanes.
